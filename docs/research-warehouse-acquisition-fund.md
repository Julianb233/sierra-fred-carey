### Off-Market Deal Sourcing Strategies (Most Deals Happen Off-Market)

1. **Direct-to-owner outreach** — Build lists of warehouse owners from county records, then contact via mail, phone, email, and SMS. This is the primary strategy for acquisition funds.
2. **Broker relationships & pocket listings** — Cultivate relationships with 5-10 commercial brokers who specialize in industrial/warehouse in your target markets. Many deals are shown to known buyers before hitting the market.
3. **Driving for dollars** — Physically scout industrial parks and warehouse districts. Note properties showing signs of vacancy, deferred maintenance, or distress. Use apps like DealMachine to photograph and log properties on the go.
4. **Absentee owner targeting** — Filter for owners whose mailing address differs from the property address. Absentee owners are statistically more motivated to sell (less emotional attachment, may not be actively managing).
5. **Tax delinquent lists** — Available from county tax assessor offices. Owners behind on taxes are often motivated. Cross-reference with warehouse-zoned parcels.
6. **Probate & estate sales** — Properties inherited by heirs who don't want to manage commercial real estate.
7. **Code violation lists** — Properties with outstanding code violations indicate owners who may be struggling or disengaged.
8. **Auction platforms** — Auction.com, Ten-X Commercial, Hudson & Marshall for distressed commercial assets.
9. **Networking** — REIA meetings, ICSC events, local commercial RE meetups, SIOR (Society of Industrial and Office Realtors) events.

### Filtering for Small Warehouse Properties Specifically

- **Zoning codes:** Filter for Industrial (I-1, I-2, M-1, M-2 depending on municipality), Light Industrial (LI), Warehouse/Distribution (W), Commercial-Industrial (CI). Codes vary by jurisdiction.
- **Property type codes:** County assessors classify by use code. Look for "Warehouse," "Light Manufacturing," "Industrial," "Mini-Warehouse/Storage," "Flex Space."
- **Square footage filter:** 1,000-5,000 sqft range. Most platforms allow sqft filtering.
- **Building class:** Class B and C industrial buildings are the sweet spot for small funds. Class A is too expensive; Class C may need significant capex.
- **Lot size:** Look for parcels with excess land (potential for expansion or additional storage).

## 2. Owner Identification & Skip Tracing

### How to Find Who Owns a Commercial Property

1. **County Assessor/Recorder website** — Free. Every county maintains public records of property ownership. Search by address or parcel number (APN). Will show the owner of record (often an LLC for commercial).
2. **Title search** — Order from a title company ($150-300) for full ownership chain, liens, encumbrances.
3. **Reonomy** — AI-powered ownership identification that pierces LLC layers and connects to true owners with contact info.
4. **PropStream/BatchLeads** — Owner name and mailing address included in property records.
5. **Secretary of State filings** — For LLC-owned properties, search the state's Secretary of State business entity database (usually free online) to find the registered agent, members, and managers.
6. **OpenCorporates.com** — Free database aggregating corporate filings across jurisdictions.

### LLC Ownership Research Process

1. **Identify the LLC** from county property records
2. **Search Secretary of State** in the state of registration for members/managers
3. **Check registered agent** — If it's a law firm or registered agent service, the actual owner is one layer deeper
4. **Cross-reference** the manager/member names with other property records (many commercial owners own multiple properties)
5. **Use skip tracing** to find current phone numbers and email addresses for identified individuals
6. **Check for series LLCs** — Some investors use a series LLC structure where each property is in a separate series; the master LLC is the real entity to trace

### Building an Owner Database — Best Practices

- Deduplicate by owner entity (not property) so you can see full portfolios
- Track: owner name, LLC name, contact info (phone, email, mailing address), all properties owned, estimated portfolio value, acquisition dates, property condition notes
- Tag owners by motivation level: absentee, tax delinquent, code violations, long-term ownership (20+ years = potential estate planning motivation), aging owners
- Update skip trace data every 6-12 months (phone numbers change)
- Append data incrementally as you learn more from conversations

## 3. Lead Nurturing & Outreach to Property Owners

### Multi-Channel Outreach Strategy

The data shows that combining multiple channels produces the best results. A typical cadence:

**Month 1:** Direct mail (professional letter) + Cold call attempt
**Month 2:** Follow-up postcard + Second call attempt + Email if available
**Month 3:** Direct mail (different piece, e.g., BOV offer) + Call
**Month 4-6:** Monthly postcard or letter + quarterly call
**Month 7-18:** Bi-monthly mail + quarterly call + email drip

### Direct Mail Campaigns

For commercial acquisitions, use **professional letterhead**, not yellow letters. You are representing an investment fund, not a wholesale operation.

**Mail Piece Types:**
- **Professional introduction letter** — Who you are, what your fund does, why you're interested in their property specifically. Include your track record if applicable.
- **Broker Opinion of Value (BOV) offer** — Offer a free, no-obligation market valuation of their property. This is a powerful door-opener because owners are curious about what their property is worth.
- **Market update postcard** — Share recent warehouse sale data in their area. Positions you as knowledgeable and keeps you top of mind.
- **Case study letter** — Describe a recent acquisition your fund completed (price, timeline, ease of transaction). Shows credibility and demonstrates your process.

### Cold Calling Scripts & Best Practices

**Optimal calling times:** Tuesday-Thursday, 10:00-11:30 AM and 1:30-3:30 PM

**Script Framework for Warehouse Owners:**

> "Hi [Owner Name], this is [Your Name] with [Fund Name]. We're a commercial real estate investment group that specializes in acquiring warehouse properties in [City/Area]. I'm reaching out because we've identified your property at [Address] as one that fits our acquisition criteria.
>
> We're not brokers — we buy properties directly with our own capital, which means we can close quickly and with certainty. I'm curious — have you given any thought to what your exit strategy looks like for that property?"

**Key positioning points:**
- **Fund buyer advantage:** Cash offers, no financing contingency, quick close (30-45 days)
- **Certainty of close:** "We don't need bank approval — our capital is committed"
- **Flexible terms:** Can accommodate leaseback if owner needs to stay, flexible closing timeline
- **No broker commissions:** Direct transaction saves the seller 4-6%
- **As-is purchase:** No repair demands

### Drip Campaigns for Long-Term Nurture

Not every owner is ready to sell today. A 6-18 month nurture campaign keeps you top of mind.

- **Email sequence:** Monthly market updates, recent comparable sales in their area, articles about warehouse market trends, occasional check-in
- **Voicemail drops:** Pre-recorded messages delivered without ringing the phone (services like Slybroadcast)
- **Ringless voicemail + SMS:** Combine for higher engagement, but be mindful of TCPA compliance
- **Annual BOV update:** "Your property was worth $X last year; here's where the market is now"

### Relationship Building

- Join local **CCIM (Certified Commercial Investment Member)** chapter
- Attend **SIOR** events (industrial/warehouse specialists)
- Build relationships with **1031 exchange intermediaries** (sellers often need to identify replacement properties quickly)
- Connect with **commercial loan officers** who hear about distressed properties
- Network with **commercial property managers** who know which owners are burned out
- Establish presence at local **Chamber of Commerce** and **Economic Development** offices

## 5. Property Analysis & Underwriting

### Cap Rate Analysis

**Formula:** Cap Rate = Net Operating Income (NOI) / Purchase Price

**Current Market Benchmarks (2025-2026):**
- Institutional-quality industrial: ~6%
- Small warehouse (1,000-5,000 sqft), local tenants, secondary markets: 7-8.5%
- Value-add/vacant warehouse: 8-10%+ (based on projected stabilized NOI)
- NNN leased warehouse with credit tenant: 6-7%

**Rule of thumb for small warehouses:** Target acquisition cap rates of 7.5-9% to leave room for value creation and adequate returns to investors after fund fees.

### Cash-on-Cash Return Calculation

**Formula:** Cash-on-Cash = Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow / Total Cash Invested

Example for a $500K warehouse:
- Purchase price: $500,000
- Down payment (30%): $150,000
- Closing costs: $15,000
- Total cash invested: $165,000
- Annual rent (5,000 sqft x $8/sqft NNN): $40,000
- Debt service (70% LTV, 7%, 25yr amort): ~$29,700/yr
- Cash flow after debt service: ~$10,300/yr
- Cash-on-cash return: ~6.2%

Target cash-on-cash for fund investors: 6-10% before appreciation

### Comparable Sales Analysis

- Pull recent warehouse sales (last 12-24 months) within the target submarket
- Normalize by price per square foot
- Adjust for: ceiling height, loading docks, lot coverage ratio, age, condition, zoning
- Sources: CoStar, Crexi comps, county recorder (deed transfer amounts), Reonomy

### Rental Rate Analysis

- Survey asking rents for comparable warehouse spaces in the submarket
- Sources: CoStar, LoopNet/Crexi active listings, local commercial brokers
- Small warehouses (1,000-5,000 sqft) typically command **higher per-sqft rents** than larger warehouses due to limited supply and high demand from small businesses
- National average warehouse rent: $8-12/sqft NNN (varies dramatically by market)
- Flex/warehouse with office: $10-18/sqft modified gross
- Track rent growth trends: warehouse rents grew ~4.5% nationally in 2024

### Physical Inspection Considerations for Warehouses

- **Roof condition** — #1 expense item. Flat roofs common on warehouses. Get age, material, last repair date. Budget $4-8/sqft for replacement.
- **Clear height** — Ceiling height inside. 16-24 ft is standard for small warehouses. Higher = more valuable (allows racking/storage).
- **Loading docks / drive-in doors** — Grade-level doors vs. dock-high. Number and condition of doors. Critical for tenant operations.
- **Column spacing** — Wider is better (more usable space, accommodates larger equipment).
- **Electrical service** — Amperage, 3-phase power availability. Manufacturers and some e-commerce operations need heavy power.
- **HVAC** — Many warehouses are not climate-controlled. Adding HVAC creates value but is expensive ($15-25/sqft).
- **Sprinkler system** — Required by code in most jurisdictions. ESFR vs. standard. Check inspection tags.
- **Floor condition** — Concrete slab. Check for cracks, settling, load capacity. Sealing/polishing is inexpensive value-add.
- **Parking / truck access** — Adequate turning radius for trucks? Sufficient parking for tenants and employees?
- **Zoning** — Confirm current zoning allows warehouse/industrial use. Check for any overlay zones or conditional use requirements.
- **ADA compliance** — Office portions must comply.

### Environmental Due Diligence

**Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA):**
- **CRITICAL for any industrial/warehouse property** — Non-negotiable
- Cost: $2,000-4,000 for a standard Phase I
- Timeline: 2-3 weeks
- Standard: ASTM E1527-21
- Components: (1) Historical review of property use, (2) Regulatory database review, (3) Site visit/inspection, (4) Interviews with owners, occupants, local officials
- Purpose: Identifies Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs) — evidence of contamination or potential contamination
- **Liability protection:** Completing a Phase I qualifies you for the CERCLA "innocent landowner" defense. Without it, you could be liable for cleanup costs even if you didn't cause contamination.

**Phase II ESA (if Phase I identifies RECs):**
- Soil/groundwater sampling and lab analysis
- Cost: $5,000-25,000+ depending on scope
- Timeline: 3-6 weeks

**Red flags in warehouse acquisitions:**
- Prior use as auto repair, dry cleaning, gas station, chemical storage, manufacturing
- Underground storage tanks (USTs)
- Stained soil, distressed vegetation, chemical odors
- Adjacent properties with known contamination (plume migration)

### Lease Structures for Warehouse Tenants

| Lease Type | Tenant Pays | Landlord Pays | Best For |
|------------|-------------|---------------|----------|
| **Triple Net (NNN)** | Base rent + taxes + insurance + maintenance | Structural/roof (sometimes) | Credit tenants, long-term leases. Lowest landlord responsibility. |
| **Modified Gross** | Base rent + some operating expenses | Remaining expenses | Smaller tenants, shorter terms. Common for small warehouses. |
| **Gross/Full Service** | Base rent only | All operating expenses | Rare for warehouse. Sometimes used for flex/office-warehouse. |
| **Industrial Gross (IG)** | Base rent + proportional share of increases over base year | Base year expenses | Common for multi-tenant warehouse. Good compromise. |

**For small warehouses, Modified Gross or Industrial Gross leases are most common.** True NNN is more typical for larger warehouse spaces with credit tenants.

### Typical Warehouse Tenants (1,000-5,000 sqft)

- E-commerce fulfillment / small online retailers
- Contractors (electrical, plumbing, HVAC, painting)
- Small distributors / wholesalers
- Auto detailing / specialty auto services
- Small manufacturers / assembly operations
- Art studios / creative businesses
- Catering / food prep (requires health dept. approval)
- Cannabis operations (where legal; requires specific zoning)
- Self-storage conversion potential
- Last-mile delivery services

### Tenant Creditworthiness Analysis

- Request business financials (P&L, balance sheet) for last 2-3 years
- Personal guarantee from business owner (standard for small tenants)
- Credit check on guarantor
- Bank references, landlord references
- Verify business license and insurance
- For small tenants: focus on personal guarantee strength over business financials
- Security deposit: typically 2-3 months rent for commercial warehouse

## 6. Fund Legal Structure

### LP/GP Structure

**General Partner (GP):**
- Manages the fund day-to-day (acquisitions, property management, dispositions)
- Makes all investment decisions
- Typically contributes 1-10% of total fund equity (skin in the game)
- Earns management fees and carried interest (promote)
- Has fiduciary duty to LPs
- Usually structured as an LLC

**Limited Partners (LPs):**
- Passive investors who contribute capital
- No involvement in management decisions
- Liability limited to their investment
- Receive preferred return before GP gets promote
- Receive regular distributions and K-1 tax documents

**Typical structure:**
```
Fund LLC (or LP)
  |
  |--- GP LLC (fund manager) — 1-10% equity + management fees + promote
  |
  |--- LP 1 — $100K investment
  |--- LP 2 — $250K investment
  |--- LP 3 — $500K investment
  |--- ... etc.
```

### SEC Regulation D Exemptions

**Rule 506(b):**
- Up to 35 non-accredited investors + unlimited accredited investors
- **No general solicitation or advertising** (cannot post on social media, run ads, etc.)
- Non-accredited investors must be "sophisticated" (financial knowledge/experience)
- Must provide more extensive disclosures to non-accredited investors
- Self-certification of accredited status is acceptable
- **Best for:** Raising from friends, family, existing network, personal relationships
- Must file Form D with SEC within 15 days of first sale

**Rule 506(c):**
- **Accredited investors ONLY**
- **Can advertise and generally solicit** (social media, webinars, podcasts, ads)
- Must take "reasonable steps" to **verify** accredited status (tax returns, bank statements, CPA/attorney letter, or third-party verification service like Verify Investor or Parallel Markets)
- **Best for:** Funds that want to market broadly and scale capital raising
- Must file Form D with SEC within 15 days of first sale

**Accredited investor qualifications (2026):**
- Income: $200K+ individual or $300K+ joint for last 2 years (with expectation of same)
- Net worth: $1M+ excluding primary residence
- Certain professional certifications (Series 7, 65, 82)
- Knowledgeable employees of the fund
- Entities with $5M+ in assets

### Private Placement Memorandum (PPM)

A PPM is the primary disclosure document for investors. While not technically required by the SEC, it is considered **essential** for legal protection and investor confidence.

**PPM must contain:**
- Description of the fund and its investment strategy
- Terms of the offering (minimum investment, fund size, use of proceeds)
- Risk factors (extensive — typically 15-30 pages)
- Description of the GP and its principals (track record, experience, bios)
- Fee structure and compensation
- Distribution waterfall
- Conflicts of interest
- Tax implications (depreciation, pass-through deductions, K-1s)
- Subscription procedures
- Financial statements (if applicable)

**Cost to prepare:**
- Attorney-drafted PPM: $15,000-50,000
- Template services (PPMFast, Syndication Attorneys): $5,000-15,000
- DIY templates: $1,500-3,000 (risky — securities law is complex)

### Distribution Waterfall Structures

**Standard 2-Tier Waterfall (Most Common for Small Funds):**
```
Tier 1: 100% to LPs until they receive their preferred return (typically 7-8% annually)
Tier 2: Remaining profits split — typically 70% LP / 30% GP (the 30% is the "promote" or carried interest)
```

**3-Tier Waterfall with Catch-Up:**
```
Tier 1: 100% to LPs until preferred return (8%) is met
Tier 2: GP catch-up — 100% to GP until GP has received 20% of total profits distributed
Tier 3: Remaining profits split 80% LP / 20% GP
```

**European vs. American Waterfall:**
- **European (whole fund):** GP doesn't receive promote until LPs get back ALL capital + preferred return across the entire fund. More LP-friendly. Better for first-time fund managers to build trust.
- **American (deal-by-deal):** GP receives promote on a per-deal basis as each deal is realized. More GP-friendly. Standard for established managers.

**Recommendation for a first fund:** Use a European waterfall with an 8% preferred return and 70/30 split (or 80/20 to be more LP-friendly). This builds trust and makes fundraising easier.

### Fee Structure

| Fee | Typical Range | Description |
|-----|---------------|-------------|
| **Management Fee** | 1-2% of committed capital annually | Covers fund overhead: salaries, office, travel, data subscriptions. Charged regardless of performance. |
| **Acquisition Fee** | 1-2% of purchase price per deal | One-time fee at closing of each property. Compensates for deal sourcing/closing effort. |
| **Disposition Fee** | 0.5-1% of sale price | Charged when a property is sold. |
| **Carried Interest (Promote)** | 15-30% of profits above preferred return | Performance-based compensation. Only earned after LPs receive their preferred return. |
| **Asset Management Fee** | 1-2% of asset value annually | Sometimes charged instead of or in addition to management fee. Covers ongoing property oversight. |

**For a first fund targeting smaller investors:** Keep fees reasonable — 1.5% management fee + 20% promote after 8% pref is a standard, defensible structure.

### Property Management

- **Self-manage** if you have 5-15 properties in a concentrated area. Saves 4-8% of gross revenue but requires staffing and systems.
- **Third-party PM** is recommended initially. Commercial PM fees for warehouse: 4-8% of gross revenue. Lower for NNN tenants (less work).
- As the fund grows to 15+ properties, consider bringing PM in-house for cost savings and better control.

## 8. Acquisition Process Workflow

### Step-by-Step: Property Identification to Acquisition

---

**STEP 1: SOURCE**
- Pull lists from PropStream/Reonomy: warehouse properties, 1,000-5,000 sqft, in target markets
- Apply filters: absentee owners, long-term ownership (10+ years), tax delinquent, code violations
- Monitor Crexi/LoopNet for on-market deals meeting criteria
- Receive broker referrals from cultivated relationships
- Log all properties in CRM → Status: IDENTIFIED

**STEP 2: RESEARCH**
- Verify property details: sqft, lot size, zoning, year built, condition
- Pull county assessor data for tax assessed value and tax history
- Check for liens, encumbrances, and title issues (preliminary)
- Review satellite/street view imagery
- Run initial back-of-envelope underwriting:
  - Estimated market rent (per sqft x sqft = gross potential rent)
  - Estimated NOI (rent - vacancy - operating expenses)
  - Target purchase price based on target cap rate
- CRM Status → RESEARCHED

**STEP 3: OWNER IDENTIFICATION**
- Identify owner from county records
- If LLC: search Secretary of State for members/managers
- Skip trace to find phone, email, mailing address
- Cross-reference to find owner's full property portfolio
- Add owner to CRM with all contact info and property data
- CRM Status → (still RESEARCHED, owner now known)

**STEP 4: OUTREACH**
- Send initial direct mail piece (professional letter on fund letterhead)
- Make first cold call attempt within 48 hours of mail delivery
- Send follow-up email if address is available
- Log all contact attempts in CRM
- CRM Status → CONTACTED

**STEP 5: INITIAL CONVERSATION**
- When owner responds (call back, email, etc.):
  - Build rapport. Ask about the property, their plans, their timeline.
  - Determine motivation level (1-10 scale)
  - Ask about any issues with the property (deferred maintenance, tenant problems, etc.)
  - Gauge price expectations (don't lead with your number)
  - Explain your fund's approach: cash buyer, quick close, certainty, as-is
  - If interested: schedule property tour / request financials
  - If not ready: move to nurture campaign
- CRM Status → IN CONVERSATION or DEAD/NURTURE

**STEP 6: PROPERTY ANALYSIS / UNDERWRITING**
- Request from owner/broker: rent roll, operating statements (last 2-3 years), lease copies, utility bills, tax bills, insurance costs
- Perform detailed underwriting:
  - Gross Potential Rent
  - (-) Vacancy & Credit Loss (assume 5-10% for warehouse)
  - = Effective Gross Income
  - (-) Operating Expenses (taxes, insurance, maintenance, management, reserves)
  - = Net Operating Income (NOI)
  - Cap Rate = NOI / Purchase Price
  - Cash-on-Cash Return = (NOI - Debt Service) / Equity Invested
  - Project 5-year and 10-year returns (appreciation, rent growth, principal paydown)
- Schedule property inspection (bring contractor for major items)
- Get preliminary insurance quote
- CRM Status → ANALYZING

**STEP 7: LETTER OF INTENT (LOI)**
- Draft LOI with key terms:
  - Purchase price
  - Earnest money deposit (typically 1-3% for commercial)
  - Due diligence period (30-60 days for warehouse)
  - Financing contingency (if applicable) or "all cash" if no financing
  - Closing timeline (30-45 days after due diligence)
  - Any special conditions (subject to Phase I ESA, tenant estoppels, etc.)
- LOI is typically non-binding (except confidentiality and exclusivity)
- Submit to owner/broker
- CRM Status → LOI SUBMITTED

**STEP 8: NEGOTIATION**
- Expect counter-offers on price, terms, or timeline
- Common negotiation points: price, earnest money amount, due diligence length, closing date, what conveys with sale, rent credits, tenant issues, needed repairs
- Be prepared to walk away — discipline is critical for fund returns
- CRM Status → IN NEGOTIATION

**STEP 9: PURCHASE & SALE AGREEMENT (CONTRACT)**
- Engage real estate attorney to draft or review PSA
- Key contract provisions:
  - Representations and warranties (environmental, structural, zoning, tenant leases)
  - Due diligence contingency with clear deadlines
  - Title and survey requirements
  - Prorations (taxes, rent, utilities)
  - Default remedies
  - Assignment clause (fund entity may differ from contracting entity)
- Execute contract. Deposit earnest money into escrow.
- CRM Status → UNDER CONTRACT

**STEP 10: DUE DILIGENCE**
- **Phase I Environmental Site Assessment** ($2,000-4,000, 2-3 weeks) — NON-NEGOTIABLE for warehouse
- **Property inspection** — Hire commercial property inspector ($500-1,500)
- **Roof inspection** — Separate specialist if roof is aged ($300-600)
- **Title search & commitment** — Order from title company ($300-600)
- **ALTA survey** — If no recent survey exists ($3,000-6,000)
- **Zoning verification** — Letter from municipality confirming zoning compliance
- **Tenant estoppel certificates** — If tenants in place, confirm lease terms directly with tenants
- **Financial audit** — Verify all income and expense representations
- **Insurance quote** — Get binding quote for property insurance
- **Review all leases** — Terms, expiration, renewal options, tenant obligations
- If issues found: renegotiate price, request repairs, or terminate contract
- **Budget: $8,000-15,000 for full due diligence on a small warehouse**

**STEP 11: FINANCING (If Not All-Cash)**
- Commercial mortgage: 65-75% LTV, 7-8% interest (2026 rates), 20-25 year amort, 5-10 year term
- SBA 504 loan: Available for owner-occupied (less relevant for investment fund)
- Local/community banks: Often best terms for small commercial. Relationship-based.
- Private/hard money: Higher rate (10-14%) but faster close. Good for value-add then refinance.
- Fund structure: May use fund equity for 30-35% down + commercial loan for balance
- **Lender will require:** Appraisal ($2,500-5,000), Phase I ESA, rent roll, operating statements, borrower financials, personal guarantee (likely for small fund)

**STEP 12: CLOSE**
- Final walkthrough of property
- Review closing statement (HUD-1 / ALTA settlement statement)
- Wire funds to escrow
- Execute all closing documents (deed, mortgage, assignments of leases, etc.)
- Record deed with county
- Receive keys
- **Typical closing costs:** 2-4% of purchase price (title insurance, attorney fees, recording fees, transfer taxes, lender fees)
- CRM Status → ACQUIRED

**STEP 13: ONBOARD TO PROPERTY MANAGEMENT**
- If self-managing: Set up property in Yardi/Buildium/AppFolio
- If third-party PM: Execute property management agreement, hand over keys, leases, tenant contacts
- Introduce tenants to new ownership/management
- Set up insurance, utilities, vendor accounts
- Create maintenance schedule (HVAC servicing, roof inspections, etc.)
- Establish rent collection process
- Set up reserve account for capital expenditures

**STEP 14: LEASE UP / TENANT PLACEMENT (If Vacant or Partially Vacant)**
- List vacant space on Crexi, LoopNet, local commercial broker MLS
- Engage local commercial leasing broker (typical commission: 4-6% of total lease value)
- Market directly: signage on property, social media, local business networks
- Screen tenants: credit check, business financials, references, personal guarantee
- Execute lease agreement
- Collect security deposit and first month's rent
- Tenant improvement (TI) allowance if needed: $5-20/sqft depending on scope and tenant quality
- Target lease terms: 3-5 years with renewal options, annual 2-3% escalations

## Summary: Critical Success Factors

1. **Data-driven sourcing** — Use Reonomy + PropStream to build targeted lists, not spray-and-pray
2. **Persistent outreach** — 7-12 touches before response. Multi-channel (mail + call + email). Most give up after 1-2 attempts.
3. **Position as a fund** — Cash buyer, quick close, certainty of execution. This is your competitive advantage over individual investors and 1031 buyers.
4. **Disciplined underwriting** — Never overpay. Target 7.5%+ cap rates. Walk away from deals that don't meet criteria.
5. **Environmental diligence** — Always do Phase I ESA on warehouse/industrial. One contaminated property can destroy fund returns.
6. **Investor alignment** — Conservative waterfall (European style), transparent reporting, regular distributions build trust and make future fundraising easier.
7. **Concentrate geographically** — Pick 1-3 target markets initially. Local knowledge and broker relationships compound over time.
8. **Systems from day one** — CRM, pipeline tracking, and investor management software pay for themselves in organization and professionalism.
